# Global catalog breach-notification corpus

The breach-notification duties for the global-catalog regimes (E3.7): India DPDP,
Singapore PDPA, Australia NDB, Canada PIPEDA/OSFI, Brazil LGPD, and South Korea
PIPA. Each chunk records the operative statutory text or, where the precise
official wording is not reproduced verbatim, an honestly labelled summary of the
duty with its citation. National statutes are public legal material. Sources and
retrieval dates are in SOURCES.md. Normalized only by removing em/en dashes per
this repository's hygiene rule.

Chunk markers are HTML comments that open with the literal "chunk:" keyword and
carry three pipe-separated fields (the stable id, the formal citation, and a short
title). The markers themselves begin below.

<!-- chunk: India-DPDP-s8(6) | India DPDP Act 2023 section 8(6) | Intimation of a personal data breach -->
In the event of a personal data breach, the Data Fiduciary shall give the Board
and each affected Data Principal, intimation of such breach in such form and manner
as may be prescribed.

<!-- chunk: India-DPDP-Rule7 | DPDP Rules 2025 Rule 7 | Manner and time of breach intimation -->
Summary (not verbatim): Rule 7 of the Digital Personal Data Protection Rules 2025
(notified 13 November 2025) prescribes the manner and time of the intimation
required by section 8(6) of the DPDP Act 2023. On becoming aware of a personal data
breach the Data Fiduciary intimates each affected Data Principal without delay, in a
concise and plain manner, describing the breach, its likely consequences, the
measures taken, and the safety measures the Data Principal may take. The Data
Fiduciary also intimates the Data Protection Board without delay with the initial
facts, and gives the Board an updated and detailed report within 72 hours of
becoming aware (extendable on written request to the Board), including the broad
facts and circumstances, the measures taken to mitigate risk, the findings on the
person who caused the breach, the remedial measures, and a report on the
intimations given to affected Data Principals. The exact rule text is in the DPDP
Rules 2025 as notified in the Gazette of India; this chunk is labelled a summary
because that precise wording is not reproduced verbatim here.

<!-- chunk: Singapore-PDPA-s26D | Singapore PDPA section 26D | Notification of a notifiable data breach -->
Summary (not verbatim): section 26D of the Personal Data Protection Act 2012 (as
amended by the Personal Data Protection (Amendment) Act 2020) requires that, where
an organisation assesses that a data breach is a notifiable data breach, it must
notify the Personal Data Protection Commission as soon as is practicable, and in any
case no later than 3 calendar days after the day it makes that assessment. Where the
notifiable breach is likely to result in significant harm to affected individuals,
the organisation must also notify each affected individual affected by the breach,
on or after notifying the Commission. The 3-day clock runs from the notifiability
assessment, not from discovery. The exact statutory wording is in section 26D of the
PDPA on Singapore Statutes Online; this chunk is labelled a summary because that
precise wording is not reproduced verbatim here.

<!-- chunk: Singapore-PDPA-s26B | Singapore PDPA section 26B | What is a notifiable data breach -->
Summary (not verbatim): section 26B of the Singapore PDPA defines a notifiable data
breach. A data breach is notifiable if it results in, or is likely to result in,
significant harm to an affected individual, or is, or is likely to be, of a
significant scale. The Personal Data Protection (Notification of Data Breaches)
Regulations 2021 deem a breach to be of a significant scale where it affects the
personal data of 500 or more individuals, and prescribe categories of personal data
the unauthorised access, collection, use, disclosure, copying, modification, or
disposal of which is deemed to result in significant harm. The exact statutory and
regulatory wording is on Singapore Statutes Online; this chunk is labelled a summary
because that precise wording is not reproduced verbatim here.

<!-- chunk: Australia-NDB-s26WE | Australia Privacy Act 1988 (Cth) section 26WE | Eligible data breach -->
Summary (not verbatim): under Part IIIC of the Privacy Act 1988 (Cth), there is an
eligible data breach of an entity if there is unauthorised access to, or
unauthorised disclosure of, personal information held by the entity, or personal
information held by the entity is lost in circumstances where unauthorised access or
disclosure is likely, AND a reasonable person would conclude that the access or
disclosure would be likely to result in serious harm to any of the individuals to
whom the information relates. Section 26WK requires the entity, on having reasonable
grounds to believe an eligible data breach has occurred, to prepare a statement and
give a copy to the Australian Information Commissioner as soon as practicable, and
to notify affected individuals. On having reasonable grounds only to suspect an
eligible data breach, the entity has up to 30 days under section 26WH to carry out a
reasonable and expeditious assessment. The exact statutory wording is in the Privacy
Act 1988 (Cth) on the Federal Register of Legislation; this chunk is labelled a
summary because that precise wording is not reproduced verbatim here.

<!-- chunk: Canada-PIPEDA-s10.1 | Canada PIPEDA section 10.1 | Report and notification of a breach of security safeguards -->
Summary (not verbatim): section 10.1 of the Personal Information Protection and
Electronic Documents Act (PIPEDA) requires an organisation to report to the Privacy
Commissioner of Canada any breach of security safeguards involving personal
information under its control if it is reasonable in the circumstances to believe
that the breach creates a real risk of significant harm to an individual. The report
must be made as soon as feasible after the organisation determines that the breach
has occurred, and the organisation must also notify affected individuals as soon as
feasible. "Significant harm" includes bodily harm, humiliation, damage to
reputation or relationships, loss of employment, business or professional
opportunities, financial loss, identity theft, negative effects on the credit
record, and damage to or loss of property. The Breach of Security Safeguards
Regulations prescribe the content of the report. For federally regulated financial
institutions the OSFI Technology and Cyber Security Incident Reporting advisory
additionally requires a report to OSFI within 24 hours of a high or critical
severity incident. The exact statutory wording is in PIPEDA on the Justice Laws
Website; this chunk is labelled a summary because that precise wording is not
reproduced verbatim here.

<!-- chunk: Brazil-LGPD-Art48 | Brazil LGPD (Law 13.709/2018) Article 48 | Communication of a security incident -->
Summary (not verbatim, English translation): Article 48 of the Lei Geral de
Protecao de Dados Pessoais (Law No. 13.709/2018) requires the controller to
communicate to the National Data Protection Authority (ANPD) and to the data subject
the occurrence of a security incident that may create relevant risk or harm to the
data subjects. The communication must be made within a reasonable time period, as
defined by the ANPD, and must mention at least the nature of the affected personal
data, the data subjects involved, the technical and security measures used to
protect the data, the risks related to the incident, the reasons for any delay where
the communication was not immediate, and the measures that were or will be taken to
reverse or mitigate the effects of the incident. Regulation CD/ANPD No. 15/2024
sets the communication deadline at 3 business days counted from the controller's
awareness that the incident affected personal data. The official Portuguese text is
in Law 13.709/2018 on the Planalto site and the ANPD regulation on the ANPD site;
this chunk is labelled a summary and English translation because the official text
is in Portuguese and is not reproduced verbatim here.

<!-- chunk: Korea-PIPA-Art34 | South Korea PIPA Article 34 | Notification of a personal information breach -->
Summary (not verbatim, English translation): Article 34 of the Personal Information
Protection Act requires that, where a personal information controller becomes aware
that personal information has been lost, stolen, leaked, forged, altered, or
damaged, it must notify the affected data subjects without delay of the items of
personal information concerned, the time and circumstances of the breach, the
measures the data subject can take to minimise damage, the countermeasures of the
controller and its remedial procedures, and the contact point for reporting damage.
Where the breach reaches the scale or sensitivity thresholds set by the Enforcement
Decree (in outline, the personal information of 1,000 or more data subjects, or
sensitive or unique-identifying information, or a breach by external unauthorised
access), the controller must also report the breach and its response to the
Personal Information Protection Commission or a specialised institution (KISA)
within the time set by the Enforcement Decree. The official Korean text is in the
Personal Information Protection Act on the Korean Law Information Center; this chunk
is labelled a summary and English translation because the official text is in Korean
and is not reproduced verbatim here.
